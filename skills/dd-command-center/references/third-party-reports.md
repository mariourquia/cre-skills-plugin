# Third-Party Reports Ordering Guide

## Overview

Five core third-party reports are required for most commercial real estate acquisitions. Each serves a specific purpose, has a defined scope, and reveals risks that the seller's materials will not highlight. Order early -- reports are on the critical path to closing.

## 1. Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)

### Purpose
Identify recognized environmental conditions (RECs) that indicate potential contamination. Establishes the "innocent landowner" defense under CERCLA (Superfund) -- without a Phase I, the buyer inherits environmental liability regardless of fault.

### Standard
ASTM E1527-21 (current standard as of 2024)

### Scope
- Historical use review (Sanborn maps, aerial photos, city directories, chain of title)
- Regulatory database review (EPA, state DEP, local)
- Site reconnaissance (visual inspection for tanks, staining, distressed vegetation)
- Interviews (current owner, occupants, local officials)
- Report with findings classified as REC, CREC (Controlled REC), HREC (Historical REC), de minimis, or no REC

### Cost
| Property Type | Typical Cost | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Small property (<1 acre) | $2,500 | $1,800-3,500 |
| Standard commercial (1-10 acres) | $3,500 | $2,500-5,000 |
| Industrial / complex history | $5,000-8,000 | $4,000-12,000 |
| Portfolio (per property) | $2,000-3,000 | Volume discount |

### Timeline
- Standard turnaround: 3-4 weeks
- Rush (available from most firms): 2 weeks (+25-50% fee)
- Desktop review only (preliminary screening): 3-5 days, $800-1,500

### What to Look For in the Report
- **RECs identified**: Any REC triggers further investigation (Phase II). Budget $15k-50k+ for Phase II sampling.
- **Adjacent property risks**: Contamination migrates. Upgradient gas stations, dry cleaners, and industrial facilities are red flags.
- **Vapor intrusion**: Even if soil/groundwater is clean, vapor from nearby contamination can affect buildings. Increasingly scrutinized by regulators.
- **Historical uses**: Former gas station, dry cleaner, auto repair, manufacturing, agricultural (pesticides) -- all trigger concern.
- **Data gaps**: If the consultant could not access records or interview prior owners, data gaps increase uncertainty.
- **Regulatory status**: Open cases on the property or adjacent properties. Check if the state requires a No Further Action (NFA) letter.

### Red Flags
- Any REC without a corresponding NFA letter from the state
- Underground storage tanks (USTs) -- even decommissioned ones may have residual contamination
- The report says "further investigation recommended" -- this means there is a problem
- Adjacent dry cleaner or gas station within 500 feet
- Property in a known contamination corridor or near a Superfund site

## 2. Property Condition Assessment (PCA)

### Purpose
Evaluate the physical condition of the building and site, estimate remaining useful life of major systems, and quantify deferred maintenance and capital expenditure needs over a 10-12 year hold period.

### Standard
ASTM E2018-15 (Standard Guide for PCA)

### Scope
- Structural systems (foundation, framing, load-bearing walls)
- Building envelope (roof, exterior walls, windows, waterproofing)
- Mechanical systems (HVAC, plumbing, fire protection)
- Electrical systems (service, distribution, lighting, emergency)
- Vertical transportation (elevators, escalators)
- Interior finishes (common areas, representative unit/suite inspection)
- Site improvements (parking, landscaping, drainage, paving)
- ADA/accessibility compliance assessment
- Capital reserve table (replacement cost, remaining useful life, year of expenditure)

### Cost
| Property Type | Typical Cost | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Small MF (20-50 units) | $4,000 | $3,000-6,000 |
| Mid MF (50-200 units) | $6,000-10,000 | $5,000-15,000 |
| Office (50k-200k SF) | $6,000-12,000 | $5,000-18,000 |
| Industrial (50k-200k SF) | $5,000-8,000 | $4,000-12,000 |
| Retail center (100k+ SF) | $8,000-15,000 | $6,000-20,000 |

### Timeline
- Standard: 3-4 weeks (includes site visit + report writing)
- Rush: 2 weeks (+25-50%)
- Site visit typically takes 1 day (small) to 3 days (large/complex)

### What to Look For
- **Immediate repairs needed (Year 0-1)**: These are costs you bear at closing. Negotiate price reduction or seller escrow.
- **Capital reserve adequacy**: Does the reserve table fund all replacements? Is the annual reserve per unit/SF reasonable ($250-500/unit/yr for MF, $0.50-1.50/SF for commercial)?
- **Roof**: Remaining life, warranty transferability, evidence of ponding or leaks
- **HVAC**: Age of units, refrigerant type (R-22 phase-out = expensive replacement), maintenance history
- **Plumbing**: Pipe material (galvanized = replacement needed, copper = long life, PEX = acceptable), evidence of leaks or water damage
- **Electrical**: Service capacity for intended use, panel age, code compliance
- **Structural**: Foundation cracks, settlement, water intrusion, load capacity
- **ADA**: Non-compliant items that trigger upgrade requirements upon renovation

### Red Flags
- Immediate repair costs exceeding 5% of purchase price
- Roof with less than 3 years remaining life (budget $5-10/SF replacement)
- Galvanized plumbing in any building (full repipe = $3,000-5,000/unit for MF)
- HVAC systems with R-22 refrigerant (replacement cost 30-50% premium)
- Foundation issues described as "monitor" -- this means the engineer is concerned but cannot definitively call it a defect
- Capital reserve table that excludes major items or uses unrealistically long useful lives

## 3. ALTA/NSPS Survey

### Purpose
Establish property boundaries, identify improvements, easements, encroachments, and other matters affecting title and use. Required by most lenders and title companies for commercial transactions.

### Standard
2021 Minimum Standard Detail Requirements for ALTA/NSPS Land Title Surveys

### Scope (Base + Common Optional Items)
- Property boundaries with monuments
- Building footprint locations with setback dimensions
- Easements (utility, access, drainage) from title commitment
- Encroachments (either direction -- neighbor onto subject or subject onto neighbor)
- Flood zone determination (FEMA panel reference)
- Parking count and layout
- Access points and curb cuts
- Utility locations (visible surface evidence)
- Optional Table A items commonly requested:
  - Item 1: Monuments placed or found
  - Item 6: Zoning classification with setback analysis
  - Item 8: Building square footage (gross)
  - Item 11: Utilities (surface evidence)
  - Item 13: Contours (1-foot intervals for development)
  - Item 19: Wetlands flagging
  - Item 20: Offsite easements

### Cost
| Property Type | Typical Cost | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Small parcel (<2 acres, simple) | $4,000 | $3,000-6,000 |
| Standard commercial (2-10 acres) | $7,000 | $5,000-12,000 |
| Large / complex site (10+ acres) | $12,000-25,000 | $8,000-35,000 |
| Update of existing survey (within 5 yrs) | $2,000-4,000 | Saves 40-60% |

### Timeline
- New survey: 3-5 weeks
- Survey update (recertification): 1-2 weeks
- Rush: add 1 week to standard timeline (+50% fee)

### What to Look For
- **Encroachments**: Neighbor's fence, wall, or structure crossing the boundary. Or subject's improvements crossing onto neighbor's land. Both create title issues.
- **Easements**: Utility easements that cross through building areas or parking. Access easements that grant others the right to use the property.
- **Setback violations**: Existing structures within required setback zones. May be grandfathered but limit future expansion.
- **Flood zone**: FEMA Zone A or AE requires flood insurance (can be $5k-50k+ annually). Zone X (shaded) is moderate risk.
- **Parking**: Verify parking count meets zoning requirements. If count is short, zoning variance may be needed for change of use.
- **Access**: Confirm legal access to public road. Landlocked properties or properties dependent on access easements have exit risk.

### Red Flags
- Building or improvement encroaches onto adjacent property or easement
- Access dependent on revocable license rather than permanent easement
- FEMA flood zone A or AE without existing flood insurance
- Survey reveals unpermitted structures (seller may not have disclosed)
- Easement location conflicts with intended use or development plan

## 4. Title Commitment and Insurance

### Purpose
Disclose the current state of title (ownership, liens, encumbrances, exceptions) and commit to insuring the buyer's interest against covered title defects.

### Components
- **Schedule A**: Property description, proposed insured, proposed policy amount, current owner
- **Schedule B-I**: Requirements to be met before policy issues (payoff existing liens, record deed, etc.)
- **Schedule B-II**: Exceptions from coverage (items the policy will NOT insure against)

### Cost
| Component | Typical Cost |
|---|---|
| Title search | $500-1,500 |
| Owner's policy premium | $2-5 per $1,000 of purchase price |
| Lender's policy premium | $1-3 per $1,000 of loan amount |
| Endorsements (various) | $100-500 each |
| Extended coverage | Additional 10-20% of base premium |

Example: $10M purchase, $7M loan
- Owner's policy: ~$30,000-50,000
- Lender's policy: ~$7,000-21,000
- Endorsements (10 typical): ~$2,000-5,000
- Total: ~$39,000-76,000

### Timeline
- Preliminary title commitment: 1-2 weeks
- Cure of requirements: varies (30-60 days typical)
- Final policy: issued at or shortly after closing

### What to Look For in Schedule B-II (Exceptions)
- **Standard exceptions**: Survey, taxes, mechanic's liens, parties in possession. Can often be removed with survey, tax certification, and affidavit.
- **Specific exceptions**: These are the critical items. Each must be reviewed:
  - Easements: review the recorded document for scope and impact
  - CC&Rs / deed restrictions: review for use restrictions, approval requirements
  - Prior mortgages or liens: should be cleared at closing
  - Judgment liens: against seller, must be resolved
  - Lis pendens: pending litigation affecting the property
  - UCC filings: personal property liens that may attach to fixtures

### Red Flags
- Exception for an unrecorded easement or claim (cannot be fully assessed)
- Judgment lien against seller that exceeds sale proceeds
- Lis pendens from litigation not disclosed by seller
- CC&Rs that restrict intended use (e.g., no residential in proposed conversion)
- Gap in chain of title (period where ownership is unclear)
- Exceptions the title company refuses to remove or insure over

## 5. Appraisal

### Purpose
Independent estimate of market value by a licensed appraiser. Required by lenders for loan origination. Also useful for equity investors, tax planning, and dispute resolution.

### Standards
- USPAP (Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice)
- FIRREA (Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery, and Enforcement Act) requirements for federally related transactions

### Approaches to Value
| Approach | Method | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Income Capitalization | Direct cap (NOI / cap rate) and DCF | Investment property with income stream |
| Sales Comparison | Adjusted comparable sales | Owner-occupied, land, residential |
| Cost | Replacement cost less depreciation + land | Special-purpose, new construction |

### Cost
| Property Type | Typical Cost | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Small MF (5-20 units) | $3,500 | $2,500-5,000 |
| Mid MF (20-100 units) | $5,000-8,000 | $4,000-12,000 |
| Large MF (100+ units) | $8,000-15,000 | $6,000-20,000 |
| Office / Retail / Industrial | $5,000-12,000 | $4,000-18,000 |
| Special purpose (hotel, senior) | $10,000-25,000 | $8,000-35,000 |

### Timeline
- Standard: 3-5 weeks
- Rush: 2-3 weeks (+50-100% fee)
- Lender-ordered appraisals: add 1-2 weeks for AMC (Appraisal Management Company) processing

### What to Look For
- **Appraised value vs purchase price**: Lender will lend on the lower of the two. If appraisal comes in below purchase price, buyer must bring additional equity.
- **Cap rate selected**: Compare to your underwriting cap rate. If appraiser uses 5.5% and you modeled 6.0%, the appraiser is more bullish -- or vice versa.
- **Expense ratio**: Compare to your T12 analysis. Appraiser may normalize expenses differently.
- **Comparable selection**: Are the comps truly comparable? Same submarket, vintage, size range?
- **Extraordinary assumptions or hypothetical conditions**: These weaken the reliability of the value conclusion.
- **As-is vs as-stabilized vs as-complete**: Ensure the appraisal reflects the correct scenario for your financing.

### Red Flags
- Appraised value more than 10% below purchase price (financing gap)
- Appraiser used comps from different submarkets or property types
- Expense ratio in appraisal materially differs from actual T12
- "Subject to" or "hypothetical" conditions that mask current property issues
- Appraiser's cap rate selection is not supported by comparable sales
- NOI in appraisal differs significantly from your underwriting (positive or negative)

## Report Ordering Timeline

For a 45-day DD period, order reports in this sequence:

| Day | Action | Report |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Order Phase I ESA and PCA simultaneously | ESA, PCA |
| Day 1 | Order ALTA survey (or request update of existing) | Survey |
| Day 1 | Order title commitment from title company | Title |
| Day 3 | Order appraisal (once lender engagement confirmed) | Appraisal |
| Day 7-10 | Title commitment received; begin exception review | Title |
| Day 14-18 | PCA draft received; review capital reserve table | PCA |
| Day 18-21 | Phase I draft received; review for RECs | ESA |
| Day 21-25 | Survey received; compare to title exceptions | Survey |
| Day 25-30 | Appraisal received; compare to underwriting | Appraisal |
| Day 30-35 | All reports received; prepare DD summary and objection list | All |
| Day 35-40 | Negotiate price adjustments or seller cures based on findings | All |
| Day 40-45 | DD resolution and go/no-go decision | All |

**Total report cost budget**: $20,000-60,000 depending on property size and complexity. Budget 0.2-0.5% of purchase price.
