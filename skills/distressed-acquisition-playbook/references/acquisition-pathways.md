# Distressed Acquisition Pathways

## Overview

Five primary entry strategies for acquiring distressed commercial real estate. Each has distinct mechanics, timelines, pricing dynamics, and risk profiles. The right pathway depends on deal stage, capital availability, risk tolerance, and operational capability.

## 1. REO (Real Estate Owned) Acquisition

### Mechanics
The lender has already completed foreclosure and holds title. You are buying from the lender (bank, CMBS special servicer, insurance company, or agency) as the seller.

### Process
1. **Identification**: Monitor bank REO lists, servicer offerings, auction platforms (Auction.com, Ten-X, RealINSIGHT), broker listings marked "bank-owned"
2. **Initial Bid**: Submit LOI with proof of funds. Lenders prefer all-cash, quick-close buyers
3. **Due Diligence**: Typically 15-30 day DD period. Lender provides limited reps and warranties -- expect "as-is, where-is"
4. **Negotiation**: Lender's asset management team has internal valuation. Pricing is BPO-driven (broker price opinion) or appraised value. Discounts from BPO: 5-15% typical
5. **Closing**: 30-60 days from PSA execution. Title insurance critical -- confirm clean foreclosure

### Pricing Dynamics
- Lenders want to minimize hold time and carrying costs
- First 90 days on market: priced at or near BPO
- 90-180 days: 5-10% price reduction common
- 180+ days: 10-20% reduction, lender increasingly motivated
- Bulk REO portfolios: 15-30% discount for scale and speed

### Risks
- Physical condition deterioration during vacancy (vandalism, deferred maintenance)
- Title defects from foreclosure process (missed notices, junior lien disputes)
- Environmental liability passes to buyer
- Holdover tenants with uncertain lease status
- No seller representations -- you bear all discovery risk

### Best For
- Buyers with strong property management and renovation capability
- Cash or bridge-financed buyers who can close fast
- Markets where REO inventory is sufficient to build pipeline

### Timeline
- Identification to close: 45-120 days
- Stabilization post-acquisition: 6-18 months depending on condition

## 2. Note Purchase (Performing or Non-Performing)

### Mechanics
Buy the mortgage note (debt) from the existing lender rather than buying the property directly. You become the lender and control workout options: modify, foreclose, or negotiate a deed-in-lieu.

### Process
1. **Sourcing**: Loan sale advisors (Mission Capital, Newmark, CBRE Loan Sales), direct bank relationships, FDIC structured transactions
2. **Bid Preparation**: Build loan-level model with property valuation, borrower financial analysis, and workout scenario tree
3. **Due Diligence on the Loan**: Review note, mortgage, assignment chain, title, borrower financials, property condition (if access granted)
4. **Pricing**: Price as function of property value, legal costs to resolve, time to resolution, and recovery scenarios
5. **Post-Acquisition Workout**:
   - Modify loan terms (extend maturity, reduce rate, forbear) if borrower cooperative
   - Negotiate deed-in-lieu (borrower surrenders title in exchange for debt forgiveness)
   - Foreclose if borrower uncooperative (adds 3-24 months depending on state)

### Pricing Dynamics
- Performing notes: 85-95 cents on the dollar (UPB)
- Sub-performing (30-90 days delinquent): 70-85 cents
- Non-performing (90+ days): 40-70 cents depending on property LTV and state foreclosure timeline
- Deeply distressed / judicial foreclosure states: 30-50 cents
- Pricing = f(property value, foreclosure timeline, borrower cooperation, legal costs)

### Risks
- Borrower may file bankruptcy (automatic stay halts foreclosure)
- Property may be in worse condition than assessed (limited access pre-purchase)
- Chain of title / assignment issues in CMBS loans
- Subordinate liens or mechanic's liens with priority disputes
- Borrower may strip property of FF&E, HVAC, or other components pre-resolution

### Best For
- Buyers who want to acquire below direct property pricing
- Sophisticated operators comfortable with legal complexity
- Situations where borrower relationship enables cooperative workout (deed-in-lieu preferred)

### Timeline
- Note purchase: 30-90 days from identification
- Cooperative workout (deed-in-lieu): 60-180 days post-purchase
- Contested foreclosure: 6-36 months depending on state and bankruptcy

## 3. Section 363 Bankruptcy Sale

### Mechanics
When a property owner files Chapter 11 bankruptcy, assets can be sold under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code. The sale is "free and clear" of liens, claims, and encumbrances -- the cleanest title you can get.

### Process
1. **Monitoring**: Track bankruptcy filings via PACER, BankruptcyData.com, Reorg.com, or specialized advisors
2. **Stalking Horse Bid**: Debtor's advisor solicits an initial bid (stalking horse) that sets the floor price. Stalking horse receives breakup fee (2-4%) and expense reimbursement if outbid
3. **Qualified Bidder Registration**: Post deposit (typically 5-10% of bid), sign bid procedures
4. **Auction**: Conducted in court or via electronic platform. Bid increments set by judge (typically $100k-500k). Open ascending auction format
5. **Sale Hearing**: Bankruptcy judge approves highest and best bid. Objections from creditors heard at this stage
6. **Closing**: 15-30 days post-court approval. Sale order provides strong title protection

### Pricing Dynamics
- Stalking horse bids typically 60-80% of appraised value
- Competitive auctions may push to 85-95% of market value
- Single-bidder outcomes (no auction competition): 55-75% of value
- Breakup fee compensates stalking horse for price discovery work
- Speed premium: buyers who can close in 15-30 days win over higher bidders needing 60+

### Risks
- Auction competition can push price above target return thresholds
- Court timeline is unpredictable (judge schedules, creditor objections)
- Winning bid subject to court approval (not guaranteed)
- Pre-closing access to property may be limited
- Environmental and other liabilities may or may not transfer (depends on sale order language)

### Best For
- Buyers seeking clean title (363 sale order is strongest title protection available)
- Acquisitions of operating businesses with real estate (hotels, senior housing)
- Complex capital structures where bilateral negotiation is impractical

### Timeline
- Filing to stalking horse selection: 30-90 days
- Stalking horse to auction: 30-45 days (bid deadline + auction)
- Auction to closing: 15-30 days
- Total: 75-165 days from filing

## 4. Receivership Acquisition

### Mechanics
A court-appointed receiver takes control of the property when the borrower defaults and the lender obtains a receivership order. The receiver operates the property and may be authorized to sell it. You can acquire from the receiver or negotiate with the lender during the receivership period.

### Process
1. **Monitoring**: Track receivership appointments through court records, receiver firm websites (Trigild, Paramount, Richardson), and lender relationships
2. **Engagement**: Contact receiver directly. Receivers are court officers with fiduciary duty to preserve and maximize asset value
3. **Property Access**: Receivers typically grant property tours and provide financial data more readily than distressed borrowers
4. **Negotiation Path A -- Buy from Receiver**: If receiver has sale authority, negotiate PSA directly. Court approval required
5. **Negotiation Path B -- Buy the Note**: Negotiate with lender to purchase the note while receiver stabilizes the property
6. **Closing**: Receiver delivers title via court order (if Path A) or you take assignment of note and resolve (if Path B)

### Pricing Dynamics
- Receiver-managed properties are typically better maintained than vacant REO
- Pricing: 70-85% of stabilized value (receiver has operated, so financials are current)
- Lender motivation varies: if receiver is cash-flowing the property, lender is less pressured to sell
- Best pricing when receiver costs are mounting and property is still losing money despite professional management

### Risks
- Court must approve any sale -- timing uncertain
- Receiver fees and expenses reduce net proceeds to lender (may make lender more or less motivated)
- Property condition depends on how long receiver has been in place and operating budget approved by court
- Borrower may challenge receivership, causing delays

### Best For
- Properties with operating complexity (hotels, mixed-use, medical) where receiver has stabilized operations
- Buyers who want better information access than typical distressed acquisition
- Markets where foreclosure is slow and receivership provides faster path to control

### Timeline
- Receivership appointment: 30-60 days from lender's motion
- Receiver stabilization: 60-180 days
- Sale process: 60-120 days
- Total from default to buyer closing: 5-12 months

## 5. Special Servicing Negotiation (CMBS Loans)

### Mechanics
When a CMBS loan defaults or triggers a transfer event (imminent default, maturity default, covenant breach), the loan transfers from the master servicer to the special servicer. The special servicer has authority to modify, extend, foreclose, or approve discounted payoffs (DPOs) and assumption/transfer of the loan.

### Process
1. **Identification**: CMBS surveillance tools (Trepp, Bloomberg, Intex, CRED iQ) track loans by delinquency status, transfer date, and special servicer name
2. **Contact Special Servicer**: Major firms include Rialto, LNR/Starwood, CWCapital (Fortress), Midland (PNC), Wells Fargo Special Servicing
3. **Propose Transaction**:
   - **DPO (Discounted Payoff)**: Offer lump sum below UPB to retire the loan. Requires borrower cooperation or simultaneous property acquisition
   - **Loan Assumption**: Assume the existing loan (if favorable terms) as part of property acquisition from the borrower
   - **Note Sale**: Offer to purchase the note from the CMBS trust via the special servicer
   - **A/B Note Restructure**: Negotiate splitting the loan into performing A-note and hope/equity B-note, then purchase the B-note
4. **Controlling Class / Directing Holder**: The most subordinate bondholder in the CMBS stack (B-piece buyer) controls servicer decisions. Understanding who this is and their incentives is critical
5. **Closing**: Special servicer processes through PSA (Pooling and Servicing Agreement) requirements. Multiple approvals required

### Pricing Dynamics
- DPO pricing: 50-80% of UPB depending on property value, foreclosure cost, and timeline
- Note purchase: Similar to bank note purchase but more process-heavy
- Loan assumption: At par (100% of UPB) but valuable when existing rate is below market
- Special servicers are fiduciaries to the trust -- they must demonstrate value maximization
- Appraisal Reduction Amount (ARA) on the trust drives servicer's loss severity calculation

### Risks
- PSA constraints limit servicer flexibility (prohibition on certain modifications)
- Multiple layers of approval slow the process (servicer, controlling class, rating agency notification)
- Borrower and buyer interests may conflict (borrower wants modification, buyer wants foreclosure)
- Special servicer fees and advancing obligations create misaligned incentives
- Loan documents may have defeasance or yield maintenance provisions that complicate payoff

### Best For
- Buyers experienced with CMBS loan mechanics and servicer negotiation
- Situations where the existing CMBS loan has favorable terms worth preserving (assumption)
- Large commercial assets ($10M+ loan balance) where institutional process is justified
- Post-maturity defaults where borrower cannot refinance but property is performing

### Timeline
- Transfer to special servicing: immediate upon trigger event
- Servicer initial assessment: 30-60 days
- DPO / note sale negotiation: 60-180 days
- Foreclosure (if needed): adds state-specific timeline (see foreclosure-timelines.yaml)
- Total resolution: 6-24 months

## Pathway Selection Framework

| Factor | REO | Note Purchase | 363 Sale | Receivership | CMBS Special |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Speed to control | Fast (30-60d) | Medium (60-180d) | Medium (75-165d) | Medium (5-12mo) | Slow (6-24mo) |
| Title cleanliness | Moderate | Low (until resolved) | Highest | High | Moderate |
| Information access | Low | Low | Low-Medium | High | Medium |
| Pricing discount | 5-20% | 15-60% | 5-40% | 15-30% | 20-50% |
| Legal complexity | Low | High | High | Medium | Highest |
| Capital required | Full purchase | Loan balance | Full purchase | Full purchase | Varies |
| Operational skill | High (rehab) | Medium | Medium | Medium | Low (financial) |
| Competition | High | Low-Medium | Variable | Low | Low |

## Due Diligence Priorities by Pathway

All pathways require standard property DD (Phase I, PCA, survey, title, rent roll, T12). Additional priorities:

| Pathway | Additional DD Priorities |
|---|---|
| REO | Vacancy damage assessment, holdover tenant rights, title search for missed liens |
| Note Purchase | Loan document review (note, mortgage, guaranty, intercreditor), chain of assignments, borrower bankruptcy risk |
| 363 Sale | Sale order language (what transfers, what doesn't), cure costs for assumed contracts, employee/union obligations |
| Receivership | Receiver's operating reports, court-approved budget, pending litigation, receiver's sale authority scope |
| CMBS Special | PSA review, controlling class identification, ARA calculation, defeasance/YM provisions, servicer advancing status |
