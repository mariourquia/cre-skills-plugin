# Commission Calculation and Marketing ROI Guide

Commission methodologies, accrual tracking, marketing channel ROI framework, and budget allocation optimization. Worked examples use a 200,000 SF Class A office building with $72-78/RSF asking rents and a $60,000 annual marketing budget.

---

## 1. Commission Calculation Methodologies

### Office Leasing Commission Structures

**Flat percentage (most common)**:

```
Formula: aggregate_rent * commission_rate

Aggregate rent = annual_rent * lease_term_years

Example:
  Tenant: TechVenture Inc.
  Suite: 1801
  SF: 5,200 RSF
  Rent: $72.00/RSF
  Term: 7 years
  Annual rent: 5,200 * $72.00 = $374,400
  Aggregate rent: $374,400 * 7 = $2,620,800
  Commission rate: 5%
  Total commission: $2,620,800 * 5% = $131,040
```

**Tiered by term length**:

```
Standard tiered structure:
  Years 1-5:  5% of annual rent
  Years 6-10: 3% of annual rent
  Years 11+:  1.5% of annual rent

Example (same deal):
  Years 1-5: 5% * $374,400 * 5 = $93,600
  Years 6-7: 3% * $374,400 * 2 = $22,464
  Total commission: $116,064
```

**Tiered by SF (large deals)**:

```
For deals over 25,000 SF, some markets tier by size:
  First 25,000 SF:   5% of aggregate rent
  25,001-50,000 SF:  4% of aggregate rent
  50,001+ SF:        3% of aggregate rent

Example (45,000 SF deal, $68/RSF, 10-year):
  First 25,000 SF: 5% * 25,000 * $68 * 10 = $850,000
  Next 20,000 SF:  4% * 20,000 * $68 * 10 = $544,000
  Total commission: $1,394,000
```

### Commission Split Structures

```
Scenario 1: Tenant has a broker, listing broker is different firm
  Listing broker commission: 2-3% of aggregate rent
  Tenant-rep broker commission: 3-4% of aggregate rent
  Total: 5-7% of aggregate rent
  Note: rates vary by market. NYC tends higher (5-6% total);
        secondary markets may be 4-5% total.

Scenario 2: Tenant has a broker, same firm as listing broker
  Combined commission: 5-6% (no split needed)
  Internal split between listing and tenant-rep agents per firm policy

Scenario 3: Direct deal (no tenant broker)
  Listing broker commission: 3-5% of aggregate rent
  (landlord saves the tenant-rep commission)
  Some landlords pass a portion of savings to the tenant as a concession.

Scenario 4: Co-brokerage (two tenant-rep brokers)
  Total tenant-rep commission remains the same
  Split between co-brokers per their agreement (typically 50/50)
```

### Renewal Commission

```
Standard: 50% of new-lease commission rate

Example:
  Original lease: 5,200 SF, $72/RSF, 7 years
  Renewal: 5,200 SF, $78/RSF (market escalation), 5 years
  New-lease rate: 5%
  Renewal rate: 2.5%
  Renewal aggregate rent: 5,200 * $78 * 5 = $2,028,000
  Renewal commission: $2,028,000 * 2.5% = $50,700

Expansion at renewal:
  If tenant expands at renewal, new space earns full commission rate;
  renewal space earns renewal rate.
  Expansion: additional 2,000 SF at $78/RSF, 5-year term
  Expansion commission: 2,000 * $78 * 5 * 5% = $39,000
  Renewal commission: 5,200 * $78 * 5 * 2.5% = $50,700
  Total: $89,700
```

### Multifamily Commission Structures

```
Structure 1: One month's rent (most common for broker-brought tenants)
  Apartment: 2BR, $3,500/month
  Commission: $3,500
  Paid at: lease execution or move-in (varies by market)

Structure 2: Flat fee per lease
  Market range: $500-$2,000 per lease
  Higher fees for luxury properties, lower for affordable housing
  Paid at: lease execution

Structure 3: Percentage of annual rent
  Rate: 4-8% of annual rent (less common)
  Apartment: 2BR, $3,500/month, annual rent $42,000
  Commission at 6%: $2,520

In-house leasing agent compensation:
  Base salary: $45,000-$65,000/year
  Plus: bonus per lease ($100-$500)
  Plus: bonus for occupancy targets (monthly or quarterly)
  Total comp range: $55,000-$90,000/year depending on market and portfolio size

Renewal commission (multifamily):
  Typically $200-$500 per renewal (lower than new lease)
  Some portfolios pay no renewal commission (renewals are expected)
```

## 2. Commission Accrual and Payment Tracking

### Payment Schedule

```
Standard payment terms (commercial):

  Payment 1: 50% at lease execution
    Trigger: fully executed lease returned to landlord
    Payment deadline: 30 days from execution date
    Documentation: copy of executed lease, W-9, commission agreement

  Payment 2: 50% at tenant occupancy / rent commencement
    Trigger: tenant takes possession and rent commencement date passes
    Payment deadline: 30 days from rent commencement
    Documentation: confirmation of occupancy, rent payment received

  Variations:
    Some markets pay 100% at execution (Southeast, some Midwest)
    Some landlords pay 1/3 at execution, 1/3 at occupancy, 1/3 at Year 1
    Sublease commissions typically paid 100% at sublease execution
```

### Commission Dispute Resolution

```
Common disputes and resolution:

1. Procuring cause dispute:
   Issue: two brokers claim credit for the same tenant
   Resolution: document first introduction date, written authorization,
               and tour/meeting records. The broker who introduced the
               tenant to the specific property and maintained continuous
               involvement is typically the procuring cause.

2. Commission rate disagreement:
   Issue: broker claims higher rate than building's commission schedule
   Resolution: building's published commission schedule governs unless
               a separate written commission agreement exists. Always
               have a signed commission agreement before showing space.

3. Payment timing dispute:
   Issue: broker demands full payment at execution; building policy is split
   Resolution: per commission agreement terms. If no agreement, market
               custom applies. Document in writing before deal closes.

4. Renewal commission dispute:
   Issue: listing broker claims renewal commission on a deal originated by
          a prior listing broker
   Resolution: per the listing agreement terms. Most listing agreements
               include renewal protection for leases originated during
               the listing period.
```

### Channel Efficiency Rankings

```
Ranked by cost per SF leased (most efficient first):

1. Signage:        $0.46/SF  -- free lead generation, highest conversion
2. Broker events:  $0.51/SF  -- pre-qualified leads from active brokers
3. Website (SEO):  $0.71/SF  -- low ongoing cost, good conversion
4. CoStar:         $1.30/SF  -- highest volume, essential for market visibility
5. LoopNet:        $3.00/SF  -- lower quality leads, overlap with CoStar
6. Paid search:    N/A       -- zero leases (0% conversion from tour to lease)

Observations:
  - Signage and broker events produce the lowest cost-per-SF but limited scale
  - CoStar is the volume driver; essential even at higher cost-per-SF
  - LoopNet has significant overlap with CoStar; evaluate whether both are needed
  - Paid search generated tours but zero leases -- targeting or landing page issue
  - Website SEO has the best ROI for a digital channel
```

## 4. Revenue Attribution Model

### First-Touch vs. Multi-Touch Attribution

```
First-touch attribution:
  Credit the channel that generated the initial inquiry
  Simple, easy to track
  Undervalues channels that support the middle of the funnel
  Example: prospect finds listing on CoStar -> tours via broker -> signs lease
           First-touch credit: CoStar (100%)

Multi-touch attribution:
  Credit shared across channels that influenced the decision
  More accurate, harder to track
  Typical model: 40% first touch, 40% last touch, 20% middle touches
  Example: prospect finds listing on CoStar (first), tours via broker referral
           (middle), visits property website for details (last)
           CoStar: 40%, Broker: 20%, Website: 40%

Recommended approach for CRE:
  Use first-touch attribution for simplicity, but track all touchpoints.
  For large deals (> 10,000 SF), manually review the full journey and
  apply multi-touch attribution to the marketing analysis.

Attribution tracking requirements:
  1. Ask every prospect: "How did you hear about us?" (at first contact)
  2. Record answer in CRM (inquiry_source field)
  3. Track all subsequent touchpoints (tours, broker mentions, website visits)
  4. At lease execution, review full journey and assign attribution
```

### Lifetime Value Calculation

```
For each marketing channel, calculate the lifetime value of leases generated:

Formula:
  LTV = aggregate_rent * average_lease_term * (1 + renewal_probability * renewal_term/original_term)

Example (CoStar-sourced lease):
  Tenant: 5,200 SF, $72/RSF, 7-year term
  Aggregate rent: $2,620,800
  Renewal probability: 65% (industry average for Class A office)
  Expected renewal term: 5 years
  LTV = $2,620,800 * (1 + 0.65 * 5/7) = $2,620,800 * 1.464 = $3,836,851

  Marketing cost to acquire: $6,000 (CoStar cost-per-lease)
  LTV / CAC ratio: $3,836,851 / $6,000 = 639x

This ratio is extremely high for CRE because the revenue per customer
is large relative to marketing costs. The key metric is not whether
marketing is "worth it" (it always is) but which channels deliver
the best LTV/CAC ratio for budget optimization.
```
