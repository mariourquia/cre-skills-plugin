# Mueller Real Estate Cycle Model

## Overview

Glenn Mueller's real estate market cycle model maps each property type in each market to one of four phases on a cycle clock. The model is empirically grounded in decades of observed market behavior and provides a framework for timing acquisition, disposition, development, and hold decisions.

## The Four Phases

### Phase I: Recovery (Clock Positions 1-4)

**Characteristics:**
- Vacancy declining from peak but still above long-term average
- No new construction (no economic justification for development)
- Rental growth negative to flat in early recovery, turning positive in late recovery
- Absorption driven by job growth consuming existing vacant space
- Tenant leverage remains high (landlord concessions still prevalent)
- Cap rates at or near cyclical highs
- Distressed asset inventory being worked through

**Subphase Detail:**
| Position | Vacancy Trend | Rent Growth | Construction | Sentiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Declining from peak | Negative | Zero | Despair |
| 2 | Declining | Flat | Zero | Skepticism |
| 3 | Declining, approaching LTA | Slightly positive | Minimal | Cautious optimism |
| 4 | At or below LTA | Positive, below inflation | Minimal starts | Confidence building |

**Investment Strategy:**
- BUY aggressively. Best risk-adjusted returns are achieved buying in Phase I.
- Target distressed assets (REO, note purchases, receivership sales)
- Value-add acquisitions at high cap rates with renovation upside
- Lock in long-term fixed-rate debt (rates typically low in early recovery)
- Development: too early. Land acquisition for future development is appropriate.

**Historical Data Points:**
- Post-GFC recovery (multifamily): 2010-2012. Cap rates peaked at 7.5-8.5% for Class B MF. Buyers in 2010-2011 achieved 20%+ IRRs by 2015.
- Post-COVID office recovery: arguably still in Phase I in many suburban markets as of 2025-2026.
- Industrial recovery post-GFC was fastest (2010-2011) due to e-commerce structural demand.

### Phase II: Expansion (Clock Positions 5-8)

**Characteristics:**
- Vacancy below long-term average and declining further
- Rental growth positive, at or above inflation
- New construction begins (developers see green light as fundamentals justify new supply)
- Absorption strong -- both organic and expansion-driven
- Tenant leverage shifting to landlord leverage
- Cap rates compressing as institutional capital enters
- Transaction volume increasing, pricing becoming competitive

**Subphase Detail:**
| Position | Vacancy Trend | Rent Growth | Construction | Sentiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | Below LTA, declining | At inflation | Early starts | Optimism |
| 6 | Well below LTA | Above inflation | Moderate pipeline | Enthusiasm |
| 7 | Near trough | Peak rent growth | Heavy construction | Euphoria building |
| 8 | Trough, beginning to rise | Rent growth decelerating | Peak construction | Peak confidence |

**Investment Strategy:**
- HOLD existing assets. Rent growth is maximizing returns on existing portfolio.
- SELL selectively in late expansion (positions 7-8) -- capture peak pricing before oversupply hits.
- DEVELOP in early expansion (positions 5-6) -- construction costs are manageable, deliveries will hit during peak demand.
- AVOID development starts in late expansion (positions 7-8) -- your delivery will coincide with oversupply.
- Refinance to extract equity at low cap rates / high values.

**Historical Data Points:**
- Multifamily expansion 2013-2019: Rents grew 5-8% annually in top Sunbelt markets. Cap rates compressed from 6.5% to 4.5% for Class A.
- Industrial expansion 2015-2022: Rent growth peaked at 15-25% in logistics markets (Inland Empire, Lehigh Valley, Dallas). Development starts reached record levels by 2021.

### Phase III: Hypersupply (Clock Positions 9-12)

**Characteristics:**
- Vacancy increasing from trough as new supply outpaces absorption
- Rental growth decelerating, approaching zero or negative
- Construction deliveries at or near peak (projects started 18-24 months ago)
- Absorption still positive but insufficient to absorb new supply
- Concessions reappearing (free rent, TI, moving allowances)
- Cap rates beginning to expand as pricing adjusts to weaker fundamentals
- Institutional selling begins (smart money exiting)

**Subphase Detail:**
| Position | Vacancy Trend | Rent Growth | Construction | Sentiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 9 | Rising from trough | Decelerating but positive | Peak deliveries | Concern emerging |
| 10 | Rising | Flat to slightly positive | Deliveries continuing | Anxiety |
| 11 | Rising above LTA | Negative | New starts stopping | Fear |
| 12 | Rising sharply | Significantly negative | Only committed projects delivering | Panic |

**Investment Strategy:**
- SELL. Dispose of non-core assets and properties with near-term rollover risk.
- DO NOT DEVELOP. Any project started now delivers into a declining market.
- DO NOT ACQUIRE at expansion-era cap rates. Wait for pricing to adjust.
- HOLD only core assets with long WALE and credit tenants.
- Prepare capital for Phase IV / Phase I buying opportunity.
- Lock in tenant renewals with longer terms to bridge the downturn.

**Historical Data Points:**
- Multifamily hypersupply 2023-2025: Record completions in Sunbelt markets (Austin, Phoenix, Atlanta, Charlotte). Effective rent growth turned negative in oversupplied submarkets. Concessions returned to 2-3 months free rent.
- Office hypersupply (structural): Began 2020 with remote work. Unlike cyclical hypersupply, this is secular -- absorption may never recover to pre-2020 levels for commodity suburban office.

### Phase IV: Recession (Clock Positions 13-16)

**Characteristics:**
- Vacancy at or near cyclical peak
- Rental growth negative
- No new construction (negative returns on development)
- Net absorption may be negative (tenants contracting or closing)
- Significant concession packages to retain existing tenants
- Cap rates at cyclical highs
- Distressed sales, foreclosures, note sales increasing
- Transaction volume at cyclical low (bid-ask spread wide)

**Subphase Detail:**
| Position | Vacancy Trend | Rent Growth | Construction | Sentiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 13 | Peak or near peak | Significantly negative | Zero starts | Capitulation |
| 14 | Stabilizing at high level | Negative but moderating | Zero | Despondency |
| 15 | Beginning to decline | Negative, approaching flat | Zero | Disbelief |
| 16 | Declining | Approaching flat | Stirrings of interest | Hope |

**Investment Strategy:**
- BUY distressed assets. Phase IV is the entry point for maximum long-term returns.
- Target note purchases at 40-60 cents on the dollar
- Acquire REO from lenders eager to clear balance sheets
- Negotiate deed-in-lieu from over-leveraged borrowers
- Lock in long-term fixed-rate debt (rates often accommodative)
- HOLD performing assets through the trough -- selling at cyclical lows crystallizes losses
- Position 16 transitions into Phase I position 1 -- the cycle begins again

## Cap Rate Decomposition by Phase

Cap rate = Risk-free rate + Property risk premium + Market risk premium + Asset-specific premium

| Component | Phase I (Recovery) | Phase II (Expansion) | Phase III (Hypersupply) | Phase IV (Recession) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Risk-free rate | Low-Moderate | Moderate-High | High | Moderate-Low |
| Property risk premium | High | Moderate | Moderate-High | Very High |
| Market risk premium | High | Low | Moderate | Very High |
| Asset-specific premium | Varies | Low (compressed) | Moderate (widening) | High (distress) |
| **Typical range** | **6.5-8.5%** | **4.5-6.5%** | **5.5-7.5%** | **7.0-10.0%** |

Note: Ranges are illustrative for institutional-quality multifamily. Office, retail, and other asset classes will have wider ranges and different timing.

## Strategy Matrix

| Phase | Buy | Sell | Hold | Develop | Refinance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I (Recovery) | STRONG BUY | No (prices too low) | Yes (ride the recovery) | No (too early) | Yes (lock low rates) |
| II Early (5-6) | Selective buy | Selective sell | STRONG HOLD | Yes (start now) | Yes (extract equity) |
| II Late (7-8) | Caution | SELL non-core | Hold core only | No (late cycle) | Yes (peak values) |
| III (Hypersupply) | No (wait) | SELL aggressively | Hold credit tenants only | No | No (values declining) |
| IV (Recession) | STRONG BUY | No (trough pricing) | Hold and operate | No | Defensive only |

## Cycle Duration and Variability

Historical cycle lengths (trough to trough):

| Asset Class | Avg Cycle Length | Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Multifamily | 8-12 years | 6-18 years | Shortest cycles; most liquid; fastest recovery |
| Industrial | 8-12 years | 6-15 years | E-commerce compressed recent cycle; strong secular tailwind |
| Office | 10-15 years | 8-20 years | Longest cycles; current cycle may be structural (WFH) |
| Retail | 10-15 years | 8-18 years | E-commerce disruption creating permanent Phase III for commodity |
| Hotel | 7-10 years | 5-12 years | Most volatile; fastest decline and fastest recovery |

**Key caveat:** Cycles are not uniform or predictable in duration. Exogenous shocks (GFC, COVID, interest rate spikes) can accelerate or extend any phase. The model is directional, not prescriptive.

## Applying the Model

### Step 1: Determine Current Phase
For each target market and asset class:
1. Track vacancy rate vs long-term average (last 20 years)
2. Track net absorption vs new supply (trailing 4 and 8 quarters)
3. Track rental growth rate (effective, not face)
4. Assess construction pipeline vs historical avg
5. Plot on the 16-position clock

### Step 2: Confirm with Complementary Indicators
- Transaction volume and cap rate trends (position 5-8: rising volume, compressing caps)
- Lending standards (position 7-8: loosening; position 12-14: tightening)
- Developer sentiment and land pricing
- CMBS delinquency rates (leading indicator for Phase III-IV)

### Step 3: Map to Strategy
Use the strategy matrix above. The key discipline:
- Buy when others are fearful (Phase I/IV)
- Sell when others are euphoric (Phase II late / Phase III early)
- Never develop into a known oversupply (Phase III)

### Step 4: Acknowledge Limitations
- Markets are not uniform -- submarkets within the same MSA can be in different phases
- Asset quality tiers move through cycles at different speeds (Class A leads, Class C lags)
- Secular trends (remote work, e-commerce) can permanently alter cycle patterns
- Interest rate regime affects all phases -- rate spikes can push any market from Phase II directly to Phase III
