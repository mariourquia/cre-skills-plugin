# Unit Turnover Budget Template Reference

Make-ready budget templates by unit condition, line-item cost ranges, vendor coordination timeline, and ROI analysis for common upgrades. All cost ranges reflect national averages for mid-tier markets as of mid-2025. Adjust +30-50% for high-cost metros (NYC, SF, LA, Boston, DC) and -20-30% for lower-cost markets.

## 2. Vendor Coordination Timeline

### Sequencing (critical for avoiding delays)

```
Phase 1: Demolition and rough work (Days 1-3)
  Who: handyman or GC
  Tasks:
    - Remove old carpet, flooring, appliances (if replacing)
    - Tear out damaged drywall, tile, countertops
    - Address any plumbing or electrical issues found
  Dependencies: must complete before painting or new flooring

Phase 2: Mechanical and plumbing (Days 3-5)
  Who: licensed plumber, electrician
  Tasks:
    - Plumbing repairs or fixture rough-in
    - Electrical repairs, outlet additions, panel work
    - HVAC repair or replacement
  Dependencies: must complete before drywall close-up

Phase 3: Drywall and prep (Days 5-7)
  Who: handyman or drywall contractor
  Tasks:
    - Patch and skim coat drywall
    - Repair or replace trim, baseboards
    - Sand and prep surfaces for paint
  Dependencies: must complete before painting

Phase 4: Paint (Days 7-10)
  Who: painter or DIY
  Tasks:
    - Prime (if needed: stains, dark colors, new drywall)
    - 2 coats on walls (use a single neutral color for all units:
      Benjamin Moore Simply White, SW Agreeable Gray, or similar)
    - 1-2 coats on trim and doors (semi-gloss white)
    - Ceilings (flat white, only if stained)
  Dependencies: must complete before flooring install

Phase 5: Flooring (Days 10-12)
  Who: flooring installer
  Tasks:
    - Install LVP, carpet, or refinish hardwood
    - Install transition strips at doorways
  Dependencies: paint must be complete (avoid paint drips on new floor)

Phase 6: Fixtures and finish (Days 12-14)
  Who: handyman, plumber (for faucets), electrician (for fixtures)
  Tasks:
    - Install new light fixtures
    - Install faucets, shower heads
    - Install cabinet hardware
    - Install blinds
    - Caulk all wet areas (tub, shower, sinks, countertop-to-wall)
    - Install outlet and switch covers

Phase 7: Appliances (Days 14-15)
  Who: delivery crew + handyman for connection
  Tasks:
    - Deliver and install appliances
    - Verify all connections (water, gas, electric)
    - Test all appliances
    - Remove packaging

Phase 8: Cleaning (Days 15-16)
  Who: professional cleaning crew
  Tasks:
    - Construction dust removal (if heavy rehab)
    - Full deep clean per move-in standard
    - Window cleaning (interior)
    - Final walk-through inspection

Phase 9: Final inspection and listing (Day 16-17)
  Who: owner/PM
  Tasks:
    - Walk every room with checklist
    - Test every appliance, fixture, outlet, window, lock
    - Take listing photos (clean, well-lit, no personal items)
    - List unit on all platforms
    - Rekey locks (if not done earlier)
```

### Vendor Scheduling Tips

```
Common coordination failures and how to avoid them:

1. Painter arrives before drywall is done
   Fix: do not schedule painter until you personally verify drywall is complete

2. Flooring installed before paint (paint drips on new floor)
   Fix: always paint first, floor second -- no exceptions

3. Appliance delivery when no one is on-site
   Fix: schedule delivery for a specific window, be present, verify connections

4. Plumber and electrician scheduled same day in small unit
   Fix: stagger by half-day -- plumber morning, electrician afternoon

5. Cleaning crew arrives while fixtures are still being installed
   Fix: cleaning is ALWAYS last -- do not schedule until all work is confirmed done

6. Material not ordered in time (backordered LVP, wrong cabinet size)
   Fix: order all materials within 24 hours of scoping the make-ready,
   confirm delivery dates before scheduling installers
```

## 3. Upgrade ROI Analysis

### When to Spend More on a Turnover

```
Decision framework: will this upgrade increase rent enough to pay
for itself within 24 months?

Formula:
  Monthly rent increase from upgrade: $[increase]
  Cost of upgrade: $[cost]
  Payback period: cost / increase (in months)
  Target: payback within 24 months (2 lease cycles)

Upgrade ROI Table (national averages for mid-tier 2BR):

| Upgrade | Cost | Expected Rent Increase | Payback (months) | Worth It? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LVP flooring (replace carpet) | $2,500 | $50-$100/mo | 25-50 mo | Maybe (consider longevity) |
| Kitchen countertops (laminate) | $1,000 | $25-$50/mo | 20-40 mo | Borderline |
| Kitchen countertops (quartz) | $2,500 | $75-$125/mo | 20-33 mo | Yes (in mid+ markets) |
| New appliances (full set) | $2,000 | $25-$50/mo | 40-80 mo | Only if old ones are failing |
| Stainless steel appliances | $2,500 | $50-$75/mo | 33-50 mo | Maybe (market dependent) |
| Bathroom vanity + fixtures | $600 | $25-$50/mo | 12-24 mo | YES |
| Light fixtures (modern) | $300 | $15-$25/mo | 12-20 mo | YES |
| Fresh paint (full unit) | $800 | $0-$25/mo | 32+ mo | Do it regardless (standard) |
| Washer/dryer in unit | $1,500 | $75-$150/mo | 10-20 mo | YES (top ROI upgrade) |
| Smart thermostat | $150 | $10-$15/mo | 10-15 mo | YES |
| USB outlet combo | $25/outlet | $0-$5/mo | N/A | Do it (cheap, modern feel) |
| Ceiling fan (bedroom) | $150 | $10-$15/mo | 10-15 mo | YES |

Top 5 upgrades by ROI:
  1. In-unit washer/dryer (10-20 month payback)
  2. Smart thermostat (10-15 month payback)
  3. Bathroom vanity + fixtures (12-24 month payback)
  4. Modern light fixtures (12-20 month payback)
  5. Ceiling fan in bedroom (10-15 month payback)

Bottom 3 upgrades by ROI (not worth it purely for rent increase):
  1. New appliances when old ones work (40-80 month payback)
  2. Fresh paint (mandatory regardless of ROI -- standard of care)
  3. LVP flooring (long payback but saves on future carpet replacements)
```

### Market-Specific Considerations

```
Class A / luxury market:
  Tenants expect: quartz countertops, stainless appliances, LVP or
  hardwood, modern fixtures, in-unit W/D, smart home features
  Skimping on finishes will result in longer vacancy and lower rent
  Budget accordingly -- $5,000-$8,000 per turn for a 2BR is normal

Class B market (workforce housing):
  Tenants value: clean, functional, well-maintained
  Focus on: cleanliness, fresh paint, working appliances, good flooring
  Do NOT over-improve (quartz countertops in a $1,200/month unit
  will not generate enough rent increase to justify the cost)
  Budget: $2,000-$4,000 per turn for a 2BR

Class C market (value/affordable):
  Tenants need: safe, clean, habitable
  Focus on: habitability items first (plumbing, electrical, HVAC, locks)
  Cosmetic upgrades have minimal rent impact in this segment
  Budget: $1,000-$2,500 per turn for a 2BR
```

## 4. Vacancy Cost Calculator

```
VACANCY COST WORKSHEET

Monthly rent: $____________ (A)
Daily vacancy cost: $____________ (A / 30) (B)

Turnover scenario:
  Make-ready days: _______ (C)
  Marketing/showing days: _______ (D)
  Total vacant days: _______ (C + D = E)
  Vacancy cost: _______ (B * E = F)

Make-ready cost: $_______ (G)
Total turnover cost: $_______ (F + G = H)

Example (2BR, $1,800/month):
  Daily vacancy cost: $60
  Light refresh (10 days make-ready + 14 days marketing): 24 days
  Vacancy cost: 24 * $60 = $1,440
  Make-ready cost: $1,200
  Total turnover cost: $2,640

  Moderate rehab (21 days + 14 days marketing): 35 days
  Vacancy cost: 35 * $60 = $2,100
  Make-ready cost: $4,000
  Total turnover cost: $6,100

  Heavy rehab (40 days + 14 days marketing): 54 days
  Vacancy cost: 54 * $60 = $3,240
  Make-ready cost: $15,000
  Total turnover cost: $18,240

Implications:
  - Every day saved on turnover is worth $60 (at $1,800/mo rent)
  - A 5-day delay in starting make-ready costs $300 in lost rent
  - Overlapping marketing with make-ready (list as "available [date]")
    can reduce vacant days significantly
  - Offering existing tenants renewal at slightly below market saves
    the full turnover cost ($2,640-$18,240 depending on condition)
```

## 5. Capital Expenditure vs Repair Classification

```
For tax purposes, know the difference:

REPAIR (deductible in current year, Schedule E line 14):
  - Fixing a leaky faucet
  - Patching drywall
  - Replacing a broken window
  - Repairing a garbage disposal
  - Painting a unit (same color or neutral refresh)
  - Replacing a broken toilet
  - Fixing HVAC (repair, not replacement)
  - Re-caulking
  - Replacing individual appliance (same quality)

CAPITAL IMPROVEMENT (depreciated over useful life, not fully deductible in year 1):
  - Adding a room or space
  - Replacing the roof
  - HVAC replacement (new system, not repair)
  - New plumbing system
  - New electrical system
  - Kitchen renovation (new cabinets, countertops, layout change)
  - Bathroom renovation (new tile, vanity, tub -- if comprehensive)
  - New flooring throughout (as part of renovation, not simple replacement)
  - Adding washer/dryer hookups
  - Adding a deck or patio
  - Replacing all windows

Depreciation periods (common):
  Residential rental property (building): 27.5 years
  Land improvements (fencing, parking, landscaping): 15 years
  Appliances: 5 years
  Carpeting: 5 years
  Furniture: 7 years
  Roof: 27.5 years (component of building)
  HVAC system: 27.5 years (component of building)
  Flooring (LVP, hardwood): 27.5 years if permanent, 5-7 years if
    considered personal property (consult CPA)

IMPORTANT: the safe harbor for small taxpayers (SHST) allows deductions
for improvements up to the LESSER of $10,000 or 2% of the unadjusted
basis of the building per year. Consult your CPA for applicability.
This can convert some improvements into current-year deductions.
```
