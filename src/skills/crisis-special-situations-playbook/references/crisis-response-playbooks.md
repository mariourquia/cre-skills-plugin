# Crisis Response Playbooks

## Playbook 1: Environmental Remediation

### Phase I to Phase II Trigger Assessment

A Phase II Environmental Site Assessment is triggered when Phase I identifies a Recognized Environmental Condition (REC). Common RECs:

| REC Type | Description | Phase II Scope |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Historical use | Former dry cleaner, gas station, auto repair, industrial | Soil and groundwater sampling at former use locations |
| Adjacent contamination | Neighboring site with known contamination | Groundwater sampling along shared boundary |
| Storage tanks | Evidence of current or former USTs/ASTs | Soil borings near tank locations, product-line routes |
| Staining/odors | Visual or olfactory evidence of releases | Targeted soil sampling at staining locations |
| Regulatory records | Site appears on CERCLIS, LUST, brownfields databases | Scope based on regulatory file review |

### Remediation Approach Decision Tree

```
Phase II confirms contamination
    |
    v
Determine contamination type and extent
    |
    +-- Petroleum (heating oil, gasoline, diesel)
    |       +-- Soil only: excavation and disposal (fastest, most predictable)
    |       +-- Groundwater: pump-and-treat or in-situ bioremediation
    |       +-- Vapor intrusion risk: sub-slab depressurization system
    |
    +-- Chlorinated solvents (TCE, PCE from dry cleaning, degreasing)
    |       +-- Soil: excavation if accessible; chemical oxidation if deep
    |       +-- Groundwater: in-situ chemical reduction, permeable reactive barrier
    |       +-- Vapor intrusion: active or passive mitigation required
    |       +-- WARNING: DNAPL solvents sink and spread. Remediation timeline: 5-20 years
    |
    +-- Metals (lead, arsenic, chromium)
    |       +-- Soil: excavation or stabilization/solidification
    |       +-- Groundwater: pump-and-treat, permeable reactive barrier
    |       +-- Low mobility in most soils; focus on source removal
    |
    +-- Asbestos (building materials, not soil)
            +-- ACM in good condition: management in place (O&M plan)
            +-- ACM damaged or renovation planned: abatement by licensed contractor
            +-- NESHAP notification required 10 days before demolition/renovation
```

### Regulatory Agency Coordination

1. **Voluntary Cleanup Program (VCP)**: Most states offer VCP with regulatory oversight, liability protection upon completion. Preferred path for acquisitions.
2. **Required reporting**: Spills and releases above reportable quantities must be reported within 24 hours (varies by state). Do not delay.
3. **NFA (No Further Action) letter process**:
   - Complete remediation to applicable standards (residential or commercial/industrial)
   - Submit Remedial Action Report with analytical data
   - Agency review (60-180 days depending on state backlog)
   - Agency issues NFA or requests additional work
   - NFA provides regulatory closure but does not eliminate all liability

### Cost Recovery

- **Responsible party claims**: If contamination originated from prior owner or tenant, pursue cost recovery under CERCLA Section 107 or state equivalent
- **Insurance archaeology**: Review historical policies (1960s-1980s CGL policies often included pollution coverage before the absolute pollution exclusion). Engage an insurance archaeologist for properties with long operating histories.
- **Brownfield tax incentives**: Section 198 allows deduction of remediation costs in the year incurred (check current status -- has been extended multiple times)
- **State brownfield incentives**: Many states offer tax credits, grants, or low-interest loans for brownfield remediation

### Budget Guidelines

| Contamination Type | Soil Only (typical) | Soil + Groundwater | Timeline |
|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|----------|
| Petroleum (small release) | $50K-$200K | $150K-$500K | 1-3 years |
| Petroleum (large/LUST) | $200K-$1M | $500K-$3M | 3-7 years |
| Chlorinated solvents | $100K-$500K | $500K-$5M+ | 5-20 years |
| Metals (localized) | $50K-$300K | $200K-$1M | 1-5 years |
| Asbestos (building abatement) | $5-$25/SF | N/A | Weeks-months |

**Rule of thumb**: Budget 2-3x the initial remediation estimate. Underground contamination is almost always larger than initially delineated.

## Playbook 2: Condemnation / Eminent Domain

### Just Compensation Framework

The Fifth Amendment (and state equivalents) requires "just compensation" for property taken for public use. Just compensation = fair market value of the property taken on the date of taking.

### Partial vs. Total Taking

**Total taking**: Government acquires entire property.
- Compensation = FMV of entire property before taking
- Owner relocates; government pays reasonable relocation costs

**Partial taking**: Government acquires portion of property (common for road widening, utility easements).
- Compensation = (FMV before taking) - (FMV after taking, considering the remainder)
- "Severance damages": diminution in value of the remaining property due to the taking
- "Special benefits": if the public project increases value of the remainder, may offset severance damages (varies by state)

### Process Timeline

| Step | Typical Timeline | Action Required |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Notice of intent / preliminary contact | T-0 | Engage eminent domain counsel immediately |
| Government appraisal | T + 30-90 days | Do not grant access without written scope limitations |
| Written offer | T + 60-120 days | Review with counsel; this is the starting point for negotiation |
| Negotiation period | 30-90 days after offer | Commission independent appraisal; prepare counter-offer |
| Filing of condemnation action | If negotiation fails | Respond within 20-30 days (varies by jurisdiction) |
| Deposit of estimated compensation | At filing | Owner may withdraw deposit without prejudice in most states |
| Discovery and depositions | 60-180 days | Prepare appraiser and expert witnesses |
| Trial / hearing | 6-24 months after filing | Jury trial on just compensation in most states |
| Appeal | 30-60 days after judgment | Evaluate cost-benefit of appeal |

### Owner Response Checklist

- [ ] Engage eminent domain attorney within 48 hours of first contact
- [ ] Commission independent MAI appraisal (do not rely on government appraiser)
- [ ] Document all property features that contribute value (not just physical, but business value, access, visibility, parking)
- [ ] If partial taking: document all impacts on remainder (access changes, parking loss, signage visibility, traffic pattern changes)
- [ ] Review lease obligations: are you required to notify tenants? Do leases contain condemnation clauses allocating proceeds?
- [ ] Notify lender: mortgage likely requires notice and may require proceeds to be applied to debt
- [ ] Notify insurer: some policies have condemnation endorsements
- [ ] Document pre-taking condition with photographs, video, surveys
- [ ] Do not accept first offer. Government appraisals are frequently 20-40% below true FMV.

### Inverse Condemnation

When government action (without formal condemnation) effectively takes property value: flooding from government project, regulatory restrictions eliminating all economically beneficial use, denial of access.

- File claim for inverse condemnation
- Burden on owner to prove taking occurred
- Must show government action, not just regulation, caused the loss
- Statute of limitations: typically 2-6 years from the taking event

## Playbook 3: Tenant Bankruptcy

### Immediate Actions (Within 48 Hours of Filing)

1. **Identify the chapter**: Chapter 7 (liquidation) vs. Chapter 11 (reorganization) vs. Chapter 13 (individual)
2. **Automatic stay is in effect**: Do not attempt to collect rent, terminate lease, change locks, or sue the tenant. Violations of the automatic stay result in sanctions.
3. **File proof of claim**: Prepetition amounts owed (rent, CAM, damages). Bar date is typically 90 days after first meeting of creditors.
4. **Engage bankruptcy counsel**: This is a specialized practice area. General real estate counsel is insufficient.
5. **Review lease**: Identify all prepetition and postpetition obligations, security deposit status, letter of credit draw rights.

### Section 365: Lease Rejection or Assumption

The debtor (or trustee) has the right to assume or reject the lease. Key timeline:

| Event | Deadline | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Non-residential lease assumption/rejection | 120 days from petition date (court may extend to 210 days max) | If not assumed within deadline, lease is deemed rejected |
| Cure of defaults upon assumption | Must cure all defaults (monetary and non-monetary) or provide adequate assurance | Includes back rent, CAM, real estate taxes |
| Assignment after assumption | Debtor may assign even if lease prohibits it | Assignee must demonstrate adequate assurance of future performance |

### Landlord Claims Hierarchy

| Claim Type | Priority | Cap |
|------------|----------|-----|
| Administrative claim (post-petition rent) | 1st (ahead of unsecured) | Actual obligation under the lease |
| Prepetition rent and damages (rejection) | General unsecured | Limited to greater of 1 year's rent or 15% of remaining term (max 3 years) per Section 502(b)(6) |
| Security deposit | Offset against prepetition claim | Must comply with state security deposit law |
| Preference recovery | Trustee may claw back payments made 90 days pre-petition | Defend: ordinary course of business, contemporaneous exchange, new value |

### Preference Avoidance Defense

If the trustee demands return of rent payments made within 90 days before filing:

**Defenses available**:
1. **Ordinary course of business**: Payments were consistent with historical payment pattern and industry norms
2. **Contemporaneous exchange**: Rent paid at or near the time it was due, in exchange for continued occupancy
3. **New value**: After receiving the alleged preference, landlord provided new value (continued occupancy) that was not secured

**Documentation to preserve**: Payment history for 2+ years before filing, lease terms, correspondence showing payments were routine.

## Playbook 4: Partnership Dispute

### Deadlock Resolution Mechanisms

Most operating agreements or JV agreements include one or more of these mechanisms:

**Buy-sell (shotgun clause)**:
1. One partner names a price for the entire venture
2. The other partner must either buy at that price or sell at that price
3. Forces market-clearing price because the offeror risks being bought out at their own price
4. Typically 30-90 day exercise period

**Texas shootout (sealed bid)**:
1. Both partners submit sealed bids
2. Highest bidder acquires the other's interest at the highest bid price
3. Avoids the "first mover" disadvantage of shotgun clause

**ROFO / ROFR**:
- Right of First Offer: selling partner must offer to other partner first
- Right of First Refusal: selling partner obtains third-party offer, other partner may match

**Forced sale**:
1. If partners cannot agree on buy-sell, either partner may force a sale of the entire asset
2. Usually requires 180-day marketing period with agreed-upon broker
3. Both partners receive proportional proceeds per waterfall

### Arbitration vs. Litigation Decision Matrix

| Factor | Arbitration | Litigation |
|--------|------------|------------|
| Speed | Faster (6-12 months) | Slower (18-36 months) |
| Cost | Moderate (arbitrator fees + legal) | Higher (discovery, motion practice, trial) |
| Confidentiality | Private proceedings | Public record |
| Appeal rights | Very limited | Full appellate review |
| Discovery | Limited | Extensive |
| Injunctive relief | Available but enforcement requires court | Directly enforceable |
| Best when | Facts are straightforward, speed matters, confidentiality important | Complex facts requiring discovery, need for precedent, injunctive relief critical |

## Playbook 5: Fund Wind-Down

### Liquidation Waterfall (Typical Closed-End Fund)

```
Step 1: Pay fund expenses (audit, legal, admin, wind-down costs)
Step 2: Pay management fees through termination date
Step 3: Return LP capital contributions (pro rata)
Step 4: Pay LP preferred return (typically 8% IRR on unreturned capital)
Step 5: GP catch-up (typically 20% of distributions until GP has received its promote share)
Step 6: Remaining proceeds split 80/20 (LP/GP) or per LPA terms
```

### Dissolution Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Key Actions |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Notice & authorization** | 30-60 days | GP notice to LPs, LPAC approval if required by LPA |
| **Asset disposition** | 6-24 months | Orderly liquidation of portfolio assets |
| **Final accounting** | 60-90 days after last disposition | Complete capital account reconciliation |
| **Final distribution** | 30 days after final accounting | Wire final distributions with detailed statement |
| **Tax filings** | Per filing deadlines | Final K-1s, final fund tax returns |
| **Entity dissolution** | 30-90 days | State filings (certificate of cancellation/dissolution) |
| **Tail period** | 2-3 years | Maintain liability insurance tail coverage |

### State Filing Requirements

- **Delaware LP**: File Certificate of Cancellation with Secretary of State ($200 filing fee)
- **Delaware LLC**: File Certificate of Cancellation with Secretary of State ($200 filing fee)
- **Other states**: File dissolution/withdrawal in every state where registered (foreign qualification cancellation)
- **Tax clearance**: Some states require tax clearance certificate before dissolution (NJ, NY, CA among others)

## Playbook 6: Side Letter Negotiation

### MFN (Most Favored Nation) Clause Mechanics

MFN clauses give qualifying LPs the right to receive any more favorable terms granted to other LPs. Critical to understand scope:

**Typical MFN scope**:
- Applies to LPs with commitments at or above a threshold (e.g., $25M+)
- Covers economic terms (fee discounts, co-invest rights, reduced carry)
- May exclude operational terms (reporting frequency, LPAC seat)
- Usually excludes terms granted to GP affiliates, seed investors, or strategic investors
- Exercise period: 30-60 days after notification

**MFN cascade risk**: Granting a fee discount to one LP triggers MFN notification to all MFN-eligible LPs. If 10 LPs have MFN rights and 6 elect the discounted fee, revenue impact is 6x the individual discount.

### Common LP Requests by Type

| LP Type | Typical Requests | GP Sensitivity |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Pension fund** | Fee discount, co-invest allocation, LPAC seat, enhanced reporting, ESG reporting | Moderate -- pensions are anchor LPs |
| **Endowment** | Fee discount, key-person provision modifications, excuse rights for certain investments | Moderate |
| **Sovereign wealth** | Tax structuring (blocker entities), confidentiality protections, ITAR/CFIUS compliance | High -- may require structural changes |
| **Fund-of-funds** | Full transparency/look-through, secondary transfer rights, discounted fees on re-up | Low-moderate |
| **Family office** | Co-invest priority, direct communication with deal team, bespoke reporting | Low -- typically smaller commitment |
| **Insurance company** | Regulatory reporting (Schedule BA), NAIC compliance, asset classification support | Moderate -- specific format requirements |

### Compliance Monitoring

After side letters are executed, ongoing compliance requires:

1. **Side letter registry**: Central database of all provisions by LP, organized by topic
2. **MFN tracking**: Log which terms trigger MFN, which LPs have been notified, which have elected
3. **Reporting obligations**: Some side letters require additional or modified reporting. Track in reporting calendar.
4. **Excuse/exclude monitoring**: If LP has excuse rights for certain investments, track elections before each capital call
5. **Transfer restrictions**: Some side letters modify transfer provisions. Track before approving any LP transfers.
6. **Annual review**: Review all side letters annually for compliance; present summary to LPAC.
