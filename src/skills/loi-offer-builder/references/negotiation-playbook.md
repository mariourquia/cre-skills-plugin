# LOI Negotiation Playbook: Non-Price Levers and Seller Psychology

---

## USAGE NOTES

Price is one variable. Experienced buyers win deals by structuring the nine other variables in the LOI to maximize their attractiveness to a specific seller type while preserving optionality and managing risk. This playbook covers the ten non-price negotiation levers and seller psychology profiles for the five most common seller types in CRE.

---

## Part 1: Ten Non-Price Negotiation Levers

### Lever 1: Due Diligence Period Length

**What it controls**: Time to inspect, underwrite, and confirm the asset before capital goes at risk.

**Buyer-favorable position**: 45-60 days, multiple extensions, free-look period within the first 14 days.

**Seller-favorable position**: 15-21 days, no extensions, deposit goes hard on Day 1.

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Shortening DD from 45 to 21 days is worth $100K-$300K on a $10M deal in a competitive process. Sellers price certainty of close.
- Offering a short DD period is only credible if you have pre-existing market knowledge. If you need to commission a Phase I ESA (takes 3-4 weeks), a 21-day DD is performative.
- Extension options cost almost nothing to the seller but are valuable insurance for the buyer. Frame as "We don't expect to use it; this is protection against third-party report delays, not buyer indecision."

**Tactical moves**:
- Pre-order Phase I, appraisal, and PCA before PSA execution to compress DD timeline credibly.
- Offer 21-day DD with a 10-day automatic extension triggered only by third-party report delays (environmental, survey, title). Seller gets the short headline number; buyer gets actual protection.

---

### Lever 2: Earnest Money Sizing and Structure

**What it controls**: Capital at risk and signal of buyer seriousness.

**Standard ranges by deal size**:
- Under $5M: 3-5% ($150K-$250K)
- $5M-$25M: 2-3% ($100K-$750K)
- $25M-$100M: 1-2% ($250K-$2M)
- Over $100M: 0.5-1% ($500K-$1M+)

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Earnest money is the most visible signal of commitment. A $500K deposit on a $12M deal (4.2%) says "we are closing this." A $120K deposit (1%) says "we're still deciding."
- Day-one hard deposits are the nuclear option. They eliminate buyer optionality entirely. Only use when: (a) you've already completed informal DD, (b) you know the asset, (c) you're competing against 5+ offers.
- Split-deposit structures: $200K refundable during DD + $400K going hard at DD expiration = seller gets $600K total commitment; buyer retains optionality during DD.

**Tactical moves**:
- Offer above-market earnest money (3-4%) with a refundable DD period instead of below-market earnest money that goes hard immediately. You signal seriousness while preserving your exit.
- "Springing hard money": deposit goes hard only upon Buyer's affirmative election to proceed at end of DD, rather than automatically. Same timeline, different default.

---

### Lever 3: Financing Contingency

**What it controls**: Whether the buyer can walk away if financing falls through.

**Buyer-favorable**: Full financing contingency through closing with right to terminate and full refund.

**Seller-favorable**: No financing contingency. Buyer closes with cash if debt falls through.

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Waiving the financing contingency is the single most powerful non-price lever in a competitive bid. It converts a conditional offer into a near-certain close.
- Only waive if: (a) you have sufficient cash/equity to close without debt, (b) your lender has provided a term sheet (not just a verbal), (c) you understand the spread between your all-cash return and your levered return and are willing to accept the all-cash number.
- A "financing confirmation" deadline (shorter than the closing date) is a compromise: buyer retains contingency for 30 days, then waives it, giving seller certainty for the remaining 30 days to close.

**Tactical moves**:
- Include a financing contingency but set the expiration date 15 days before closing. This gives the seller near-certainty while protecting the buyer during the underwriting period.
- Provide a lender pre-qualification letter with the LOI. Even with a financing contingency, a pre-qual from a known lender (CBRE Capital, Walker & Dunlop, Berkadia) dramatically increases credibility.

---

### Lever 4: Closing Timeline

**What it controls**: Speed of execution and capital deployment.

**Fast close advantages**: Sellers in distress, estate situations, or fund-wind-down scenarios place enormous value on speed. A 30-day close can beat a 10% higher offer that closes in 90 days.

**Standard timelines by deal complexity**:
- Stabilized multifamily, clean title: 30-45 days
- Value-add multifamily, standard DD: 45-60 days
- Office/retail with complex leases: 60-90 days
- Land with entitlement issues: 90-120 days
- Portfolio transactions: 90-120 days

**Tactical moves**:
- "Time-kill" protection: include a provision that if Seller delays closing by more than 15 days, Buyer may terminate with full deposit refund. This protects against sellers who sign PSAs to shop your offer.
- Simultaneous sign-and-close: rare but powerful in estate sales where the executor needs speed. Pre-negotiate PSA terms in the LOI to compress the PSA negotiation phase.

### Lever 5: Access Provisions

**What it controls**: Quality and completeness of DD information.

**Critical items that sellers resist providing**:
- Unit-level rent rolls with tenant names (privacy concerns)
- Trailing 3-year operating statements (embarrassment about underperformance)
- Capital expenditure records (may reveal deferred maintenance)
- Environmental reports (may reveal known contamination)
- Insurance loss runs (may reveal claims history)

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Sellers who resist providing standard DD materials are either hiding something or unsophisticated. Neither is a reason to proceed without the information.
- Frame access requests as "standard institutional buyer requirements" rather than "we need to verify your numbers." The former is process; the latter is accusation.

**Tactical moves**:
- Request DD materials in the LOI, not in the PSA. This front-loads the information flow and identifies problem areas before you spend legal fees on PSA negotiation.
- Include a PSA provision that Seller's failure to deliver DD materials within 5 business days triggers an automatic DD period extension, day-for-day.

---

### Lever 6: Free-Look Period

**What it controls**: A window within the DD period where the buyer can terminate for any reason with zero consequences.

**Standard structure**: First 7-14 days of DD period. Buyer may terminate without cause; full deposit refund; no obligation to provide inspection reports or findings to Seller.

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Free-look periods are standard in markets where buyers submit LOIs before completing preliminary underwriting. They are less common in institutional marketed processes.
- The free-look is not about the property; it's about portfolio allocation. Buyers sometimes need 7-10 days to confirm that their equity allocation and fund capacity support the acquisition.
- Sellers view free-look periods as a sign that the buyer is not fully committed. Counter by increasing earnest money: "We'll put up $500K in earnest money with a 14-day free-look. If we're in at Day 15, we're closing."

**Tactical moves**:
- In a competitive bid, drop the free-look entirely. It's worth more to the seller than a 2-3% price increase.
- In a bilateral negotiation, request a 14-day free-look and offer to deliver all third-party reports to Seller upon termination as a concession. This costs you nothing (you've already paid for the reports) and gives the Seller future value.

---

### Lever 7: Extension Options

**What it controls**: Buyer's ability to extend DD or closing timelines if needed.

**Standard structures**:
- DD extension: 1-2 extensions of 10-15 days each, triggered by written notice + non-refundable extension deposit ($10K-$50K per extension)
- Closing extension: 1 extension of 15-30 days, triggered by written notice + additional hard deposit equal to 0.5-1.0% of purchase price

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Extension options are the most cost-effective risk management tool in the LOI. A $25K extension deposit buys 15 days to resolve a title issue, environmental finding, or financing delay.
- Sellers undervalue extension options because they assume the buyer will close on time. Buyers undervalue them because they assume they'll close on time. Both are wrong 30-40% of the time.

**Tactical moves**:
- Include automatic extensions triggered by specific events: title objection cure period, environmental Phase II triggered by Phase I findings, lender appraisal delays. These are reasonable and hard for sellers to refuse.
- Make extension deposits non-refundable but applicable to purchase price. Seller gets compensation for delay; Buyer doesn't lose the money if the deal closes.

---

### Lever 8: Assignment Rights

**What it controls**: Buyer's ability to assign the PSA to an affiliate entity before closing.

**Why it matters**: Most institutional buyers acquire through single-purpose entities (SPEs) formed specifically for each acquisition. The LOI is signed by the operating company; the PSA needs to be assignable to the SPE.

**Seller concerns**: Assignment to an undercapitalized entity that can't close. Shell entities used to flip contracts.

**Tactical moves**:
- Include "Buyer may assign this agreement to an entity controlled by Buyer's principal(s) without Seller's consent, provided that Buyer remains jointly and severally liable for all obligations under the PSA."
- This addresses the seller's concern (original buyer stays on the hook) while preserving the buyer's structural flexibility.

---

### Lever 9: Seller Post-Closing Obligations

**What it controls**: Seller's exposure after the deal closes.

**Key provisions**:
- Representations and warranties survival period: 6-18 months (market: 12 months)
- Indemnification for undisclosed liabilities, environmental issues, or tenant disputes arising from the Seller's ownership period
- Holdback/escrow for known issues (e.g., pending litigation, deferred maintenance items identified during DD)

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- Institutional sellers will fight hard to limit post-closing exposure. They want a clean break.
- Private sellers and estate sales are more flexible because the individuals involved often don't understand the exposure.
- A 12-month rep survival period with a 5% indemnification cap is market. Pushing for 24 months or 10% cap will meet resistance but is reasonable for assets with environmental or litigation risk.

**Tactical moves**:
- Trade a shorter survival period (6 months instead of 12) for a broader scope of representations. This is a win-win: seller gets a shorter exposure window; buyer gets better information.
- On distressed acquisitions, request a closing escrow (2-5% of purchase price) held by the title company for 6-12 months to cover undisclosed liabilities. Release is automatic if no claims are filed.

---

### Lever 10: Prorations and Closing Adjustments

**What it controls**: Who bears the economic risk of the property between PSA execution and closing.

**Standard prorations**:
- Rents: prorated as of closing date (Buyer gets rents from closing date forward)
- Property taxes: prorated as of closing date based on most recent bill
- Utility deposits: transferred to Buyer or credited
- Prepaid insurance: credited to Seller for unused portion
- Security deposits: transferred to Buyer with corresponding liability
- Pending capital projects: Seller responsible for work completed before closing

**Negotiation dynamics**:
- The big fight is usually over "gap rents" -- rent collections for the period between PSA execution and closing. If a tenant stops paying during this period, who bears the loss?
- Standard: Seller bears collection risk through closing; Buyer assumes collection risk after closing. Delinquent rents at closing are assigned to Seller for 60-90 days of collection, then revert to Buyer.

**Tactical moves**:
- Request a rent credit for any tenants that are 60+ days delinquent at closing. This protects against inheriting a collection problem.
- Negotiate a "true-up" provision: prorations are estimated at closing and reconciled within 90 days based on actual figures. Standard for large transactions but often omitted in smaller deals.

## Part 2: Seller Psychology by Seller Type

### Seller Type 1: Institutional (REIT, Fund, Pension)

**Motivations**: Portfolio rebalancing, fund wind-down, capital recycling, tax year timing. Not motivated by top-dollar; motivated by certainty of close, clean execution, and adherence to their internal timeline.

**Decision process**: Investment committee approval required. Broker runs a marketed process. Offers evaluated on a matrix: price, deposit, DD period, contingencies, buyer track record, proof of funds.

**What they value most** (in order):
1. Certainty of close (proof of funds, no financing contingency)
2. Clean execution (experienced buyer, reputable counsel, no re-trades)
3. Timeline alignment with their fund/reporting cycle
4. Price

**What they hate**:
- Re-trades after DD (will blacklist you with every broker in the market)
- Extension requests (signals inability to perform)
- Excessive legal markups to the PSA (they have a form; take it or leave it)

**Tactical approach**: Lead with certainty. Offer a tight DD (21 days), waive financing contingency, provide proof of funds with the LOI, reference your track record by name. Price can be 2-5% below the top bid if your execution profile is materially better.

---

### Seller Type 2: Private Owner (Mom-and-Pop, Long-Term Hold)

**Motivations**: Retirement, life event (divorce, death of partner), portfolio simplification, property fatigue. Often emotionally attached to the property. May have owned for 20-30 years.

**Decision process**: Owner makes the decision, sometimes with input from a CPA or attorney. Rarely uses a structured bid process. Relationship-driven.

**What they value most**:
1. Relationship and trust (do they like you? will you take care of "their" building?)
2. Simplicity (they don't want a 60-page PSA with 14 exhibits)
3. Price (but often have an unrealistic anchor based on a peak-market comp or an inflated broker opinion)
4. Tax treatment (1031 exchange timing is critical)

**What they hate**:
- Being treated like a "deal" rather than a person selling their life's work
- Aggressive re-trades or nitpicking during DD
- Being rushed (they've owned for 25 years; they're not in a hurry)

**Tactical approach**: Build the relationship before submitting the LOI. Tour the property with the owner. Ask about the history. Compliment what they've maintained. Then submit a clean LOI with balanced terms and a personal note explaining your plans for the property. If they're doing a 1031, confirm your closing timeline accommodates their exchange window.

---

### Seller Type 3: Estate Sale / Trust

**Motivations**: Fiduciary obligation to maximize value for beneficiaries. Speed of liquidation. The trustee or executor often has no emotional attachment and wants to dispose of the asset efficiently.

**Decision process**: Trustee/executor has authority but must demonstrate fiduciary prudence. Court approval may be required (especially in probate). Multiple beneficiaries may need to sign off.

**What they value most**:
1. Speed (the estate wants to distribute; holding costs eat into the estate)
2. Certainty (the trustee's reputation is on the line; a failed closing is a fiduciary failure)
3. Simplicity (the trustee is managing 15 other matters; your deal is one line item)
4. Price (must be demonstrably "fair" for fiduciary defense)

**What they hate**:
- Complexity that delays distribution to beneficiaries
- Contingencies that create closing risk
- Requests for seller representations (the trustee may not have personal knowledge)

**Tactical approach**: Lead with speed and certainty. Offer a 30-day close with a hard deposit. Minimize seller representations (the trustee will insist on "as-is" because they lack personal knowledge of the property). Offer to fund a title insurance policy that protects the buyer instead of relying on seller reps. If court approval is required, include a PSA provision that accommodates the court timeline.

---

### Seller Type 4: Distressed / Forced Sale

**Motivations**: Debt maturity, foreclosure avoidance, capital call default, operating losses, partnership dispute. The seller must sell; the only question is timing and price.

**Decision process**: Often driven by the lender or the court. Special servicer, receiver, or bankruptcy trustee may have approval authority. Timeline is externally imposed.

**What they value most**:
1. Speed above all else (the clock is ticking toward foreclosure, maturity default, or auction)
2. Certainty (the lender/court needs a definitive outcome)
3. Cash (no financing contingencies; the buyer must close)
4. Clean structure (no complicated post-closing obligations; "as-is, where-is")

**What they hate**:
- Slow buyers who consume the remaining timeline before the debt matures
- Contingencies of any kind (the seller can't afford a failed closing)
- Requests for seller financing, seller credits, or any structure that reduces day-one proceeds

**Tactical approach**: All-cash or hard-money financed. Day-one hard deposit. 14-21 day close. Minimal DD (you're underwriting the asset before you submit the LOI). "As-is, where-is" with limited or no seller representations. You're buying risk at a discount; price accordingly. Target 15-30% below stabilized value.

---

### Seller Type 5: Developer / Merchant Builder

**Motivations**: Capital recycling for the next project. IRR optimization (every month of holding cost compresses developer returns). LP return obligations.

**Decision process**: The developer's CFO or asset manager runs the sale. Process is semi-structured: 3-5 targeted buyers, best-and-final round. Decision is fast (1-2 weeks).

**What they value most**:
1. Speed to close (holding cost on a $50M development is $200K-$400K/month)
2. Price certainty (no re-trades; the developer has already modeled their exit)
3. Clean closing (the developer's LP reporting depends on a clean disposition narrative)
4. Flexibility on closing date (may need to align with their LP quarterly reporting)

**What they hate**:
- Buyers who re-trade after DD (developers talk to each other; you'll be blacklisted)
- Extended DD periods on new construction (the property is brand new; what are you inspecting for 45 days?)
- Requests for extensive representations on a newly built property

**Tactical approach**: Tour the property, review the rent roll, and submit a firm offer within 48 hours. Short DD (14-21 days) focused on lease audit and title. Hard deposit at DD expiration. Close within 30-45 days. If the property has a lease-up warranty or trailing concessions, negotiate a rent credit at closing rather than a price reduction -- the developer cares about the headline number for their track record.

## Part 4: Common Negotiation Traps

**Trap 1: Winning on price, losing on terms**. A seller accepts your price but loads the PSA with onerous provisions: personal guarantees on environmental indemnity, 24-month rep survival, 10% indemnification cap, no assignment rights. The headline price was a concession to extract painful terms. Read every word of the seller's form PSA before you celebrate.

**Trap 2: The "best and final" that isn't**. Brokers frequently call for "best and final" offers to create urgency. In reality, they often go back to the top 2-3 bidders for another round. Don't bid your maximum on "best and final" unless you have credible information that the process is truly terminal.

**Trap 3: The re-trade accusation**. Raising a legitimate DD finding is not a re-trade. A re-trade is reducing your price without new information. If you discover a $500K roof replacement during DD, requesting a price adjustment is good faith negotiation. If you reduce your price because you "re-ran the model," that is a re-trade and will damage your reputation.

**Trap 4: Over-lawyering the LOI**. An LOI that reads like a PSA signals that PSA negotiation will be painful. Keep the LOI to 2-3 pages. Save the detailed provisions for the PSA. If your LOI is 8 pages, the seller's attorney will charge $15K to review it and the seller will wonder what you're hiding.

**Trap 5: Ignoring the broker**. The broker controls information flow, timing, and seller psychology. A 5-minute call with the listing broker before submitting the LOI is worth more than 5 hours of financial modeling. Ask: "What does the seller care about most besides price?" The broker will tell you.
